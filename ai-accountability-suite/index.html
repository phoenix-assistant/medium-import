I built four tools to hold AI companies accountable. Here's what I found.

In May 2024, I watched OpenAI's superalignment team implode in real-time on Twitter. Jan Leike quit. Ilya left. Others followed. By the time the news cycle moved on, I had a nagging question: who's actually left working on safety at the major labs?

Nobody could answer that. Not with data.

So I started tracking. What began as a spreadsheet became an obsession, which became four open-source tools. Six months later, I have data on 12 AI companies across four accountability dimensions — and the picture is worse than I expected.

The Accountability Gap

I call this The Accountability Gap — the distance between what AI companies promise and what they actually do, measured across four dimensions:

1. Personnel — Who's working on safety? Are teams growing or shrinking?

2. Policy — What did they commit to? Has it changed?

3. Compliance — Do their systems meet regulatory requirements?

4. Disclosure — When they go public, what do they actually reveal?

Most coverage focuses on one dimension at a time. A news story about a researcher leaving. A blog post about a policy update. An IPO filing that says nothing. Individually, these are data points. Together, they're a pattern.

Dimension 1: Personnel Tracking (PULSE)

The tool: ai-governance-tracker

The pattern: PULSE — Personnel tracking, Update feeds, Lab comparisons, Safety metrics, Event timeline.

I track safety team composition, researcher movements, compute allocation, and published research across major labs. The data tells a clear story: safety teams are losing senior researchers faster than they're being replaced, and the replacements are more junior.

Dimension 2: Policy Drift Detection (DRIFT)

The tool: policy-commitment-tracker

The pattern: DRIFT — Document commitments, Record snapshots, Identify changes, Flag regressions, Track over time.

This one scares me the most. I've caught multiple instances where companies quietly softened safety commitments. A "we will never" becomes "we generally don't." A concrete timeline becomes "when appropriate." Nobody notices because nobody's diffing the policy pages.

Dimension 3: Automated Compliance (R.A.C.E.)

The tool: compliance-verification

The pattern: R.A.C.E. — Regulation mapping, Automated evidence collection, Continuous checking, Exportable reports.

I spent 3 weeks manually checking EU AI Act compliance for one system. Then automated it in 3 hours. Compliance verification runs in CI/CD — every PR gets a compliance diff, every deployment gets a compliance gate.

Dimension 4: IPO Disclosure Scoring (The Opacity Tax)

The tool: ai-ipo-transparency-tracker

The finding: I read 12 AI company S-1 filings. Most said nothing meaningful about safety. Twelve of fourteen "safety" mentions in one filing were boilerplate. I call this The Opacity Tax — the hidden cost investors pay when AI companies substitute vague language for actual disclosure.

When This Does NOT Apply

  • Open-source models with no corporate entity — governance tracking requires an organization to track. Community models have different accountability mechanisms.
  • Internal-only tools with no external users — compliance requirements scale with risk. An internal productivity tool has different obligations than a customer-facing AI.
  • Research prototypes — holding research to production compliance standards kills innovation. Apply this to shipped products.

What's Next

I'm working on a unified dashboard that combines all four dimensions into a single accountability score per company. Think of it as a credit rating for AI safety — based on data, not press releases.
