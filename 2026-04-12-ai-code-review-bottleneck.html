I Approved a PR in Two Minutes. Three Days Later We Rolled It Back.

The Opacity Tax on AI-generated code is invisible until it isn’t.

I’ll be honest — I rubber-stamped it. The PR was clean. Tests passed. The diff looked like something our senior engineer would write. So I approved it in two minutes and moved on.

Three days later, a customer-facing bug slipped through. The root cause? A subtle interaction between two services that the AI had no way to understand. The code was syntactically perfect and logically wrong. And I’d signed off on it because it looked right.

That’s when I realized: AI didn’t create a code quality problem. It created a code review problem. And my team’s review process was designed for a world where authors understand what they wrote.

The Numbers Are Worse Than You Think

GitHub’s 2024 Copilot report: AI-assisted developers merge PRs 55% faster.

Sounds great. But dig in — the speed increase is almost entirely in writing. Review velocity? Unchanged. So teams generate more PRs, faster, and route them through the same human review capacity.

GitClear’s analysis of 153 million lines found AI-assisted codebases show: - 2x code churn — code written then reverted shortly after - Increased duplication — AI copies patterns instead of abstracting - Declining documentation on complex changes

The code is getting written faster. It’s not getting understood faster.

I Call It the Opacity Tax

Here’s the mental model:

Traditional code review works because of an implicit contract: the author understands what they wrote. Reviewers check for blind spots, not comprehension. The author can explain any line if asked.

AI-assisted code review breaks this contract. The author iterated with a model until tests passed. The reasoning lives in prompt history, not in their head. Ask them “why did you use a mutex here instead of a channel?” and the honest answer is “because the AI suggested it.”

The Opacity Tax is the hidden cost your team pays every time a reviewer has to reverse-engineer the intent behind AI-generated code — because the author can’t explain it either.

Here’s what it looks like in practice:

❌ Traditional review (low opacity):
   Reviewer: "Why a mutex here?"
   Author: "Because the shared state is only accessed in two goroutines, 
            and a channel felt like overkill for a simple counter."
   → Reviewer trusts the reasoning. Quick approval.

✅ AI-assisted review (high opacity):  
   Reviewer: "Why a mutex here?"
   Author: "The AI suggested it. Tests pass."
   → Reviewer must now independently verify the concurrency model.
   → 3x longer review. Or worse — they skip it.

Most teams are choosing “skip it.” That’s how subtle bugs ship.

The 4-Field PR Template That Fixed This For Us

After the rollback, I mandated a new field in every PR template. It took 30 seconds to fill out and cut our post-merge bug rate by roughly 40% in the first month.

Copy this into your PR template:

## AI Assistance Disclosure

**AI tools used:** [e.g., Copilot, Claude Code, Cursor — or "None"]

**What AI generated vs what I wrote:**
[Be specific. "AI wrote the retry logic, I wrote the error handling"]

**What I'm LEAST confident about:**
[The part you'd want a second pair of eyes on]

**Alternatives I considered (not the AI — me):**
[Forces you to think independently about the approach]

The magic is in field 3 and 4.

“What I’m least confident about” forces the author to audit their own understanding. If they can’t name a weak spot, that’s a red flag — they didn’t think critically about what the AI produced.

“Alternatives I considered” puts the human back in the decision loop. You can’t fill this out by copying AI output. You have to actually reason about the problem.

What Changed After We Adopted This

  • Review time went up 15%. That’s the honest trade-off. But it’s intentional overhead, not wasted time.
  • Post-merge reverts dropped 40%. The bugs we caught in review would have been expensive in production.
  • Authors started understanding their own code again. The template became a forcing function for comprehension.
  • Junior devs improved fastest. They stopped treating AI output as authoritative and started treating it as a first draft.

When This Does NOT Apply

  • Throwaway prototypes — if nobody’s reviewing it, the template adds friction for no gain
  • Typo/config-only PRs — don’t make people fill out an AI disclosure for a one-line env var change
  • Solo projects — you’re reviewing your own code; the opacity is yours to manage
  • Teams where AI usage is < 20% — the overhead isn’t justified until AI is a significant portion of your code

This matters when AI-generated code is flowing through your review queue regularly and reviewers are struggling to keep up.

The Uncomfortable Math

Here’s the equation most engineering leaders haven’t done:

Old world: 10 PRs/week × 45 min review each = 7.5 hours of review AI world: 25 PRs/week × 20 min review each = 8.3 hours of review

You shipped 2.5x more code. Your review burden increased. And the review quality per PR dropped because 20 minutes isn’t enough to reverse-engineer AI intent.

The Opacity Tax isn’t a line item on your sprint board. But it’s in your bug rate, your technical debt, and your team’s slowly eroding confidence in their own codebase.

Start with the template. It costs nothing and it makes the invisible visible.
